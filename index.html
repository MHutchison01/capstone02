The Playbook — 8 rules from 8 practice rounds

Proof: last in profit R1 → #1 every round after · stock $34 → $175 · cumulative profit +43% vs 2nd place

  • 1 · R&D is the engine. Hit the drifted ideal every year, complete in-year (walk-back method). Exception: Low End ages to 7.0 — leave it, then one decisive reset.
  • 2 · Produce to potential, not fear. "Potential Sold" in the Courier is the truth. Best-CSS products earn above fair share. We left ~$60M on the table learning this.
  • 3 · Invest early, harvest late. Automation on volume lines R1–2; capacity by R4; after R5 stop buying and shovel cash to shareholders.
  • 4 · Price by segment psychology. Premium segments (price 9–19%): top of range, always. Low End (53%): fight — unless capacity-constrained, then RAISE price and bank margin.
  • 5 · HR + TQM compound. Spend big and steady ($1,500×3 HR, ~$1k×7–10 TQM): turnover 5% vs 13%, R&D cycle −22% — which powers rule 1.
  • 6 · Finance serves the machine. Cash ≥8% of sales (emergency loan = death). Bonds fund plant. Too profitable → under-levered → dividends & buybacks, never retire debt below 1.8.
  • 7 · Read the Courier like a predator. Stockouts = orphaned customers. Locked R&D = free share for years. New products & capacity moves telegraph strategy.
  • 8 · Process wins. Decisions hours early. Pre-save checklist: dates in-year → leverage ≥1.8 → closing cash safe → profit above bar.

Instructor intel (Robyn's hints)

  • 7 rounds, not 8 — investment window is R1–3, harvest R5–7.
  • Be bold in Round 1. Wait-and-see costs 2–3 years of catch-up. Commit to a direction immediately.
  • New product = ~2 years to build. Launch in R1 → selling by R2–3 → pays for 4–5 rounds. If we want a second premium product, it must start NOW.
  • Killing a product = ~2 years. And the fire-sale trap: when discontinuing, sell capacity down to 1 unit, never all of it (599 of 600). Selling 100% triggers forced liquidation of remaining inventory at 50% of COST. Sell the last unit the following year.
  • "Big Al" = the emergency lender. Meeting him once ≈ what happened to a team in an example game from another cohort (−$25M, $84M loan, leverage 6.2).
  • Sim rewards virtuous cycles + time lags (our HR/TQM compounding) and punishes standing still. Game theory applies — anticipate rivals, don't just react.

Research-validated mechanics (official Capsim guides)

  • The demand score formula. Customer Survey Score = base score (out of 100, from the weighted buying criteria) times [(1 + awareness) / 2] times [(1 + accessibility) / 2]. At 50% awareness a product loses a quarter of its score. At 100% it loses nothing. Pushing awareness and accessibility toward 100% on volume products is one of the highest-leverage spends in the game.
  • A/R credit terms cut the score too (official numbers): 120 days = no penalty. 60 days = 1.5% penalty. 30 days = 5% penalty. 0 days = 40% penalty. Action: set A/R to 60 days in Round 1 (recovers most of the penalty for a small cash-timing cost). Revisit 90 to 120 days later when cash is deep.
  • Scoring is monthly and relative. Customers score products every month and split that month's sales in proportion to scores. What matters is the SPREAD between our score and rivals' scores, not the absolute number. The ideal spot and ideal age move every month, so a product can only be perfect one month a year. The December score is what the Courier publishes.
  • Rough cuts zero the score: price outside the segment range, position outside the perceptual-map circle, or MTBF below the range floor. This is what destroyed a team in an example game from another cohort. Never breach a range.
  • Perfect product definition: price at the BOTTOM of the range, position at the ideal spot, MTBF at the TOP of the range, ideal age. We deliberately give up a few points on low-weight criteria (price in premium segments, MTBF in Low End) to bank margin. That trade is intentional and profitable.
  • TQM demand boost: Channel Support + QFD + CCE/6-Sigma together lift product scores by up to 10%. All three are in our TQM bundle.
  • Advanced Marketing module (only if enabled): the Sales Budget then splits into Accessibility (infrastructure, remembered year to year) and Time Allocations (this year only, up to +15% score, taken from other products). Check round 1 screens; if present, weight time toward our highest-margin products.
  • Stock price = book value + EPS + dividends. Nothing else. Dividends above EPS add nothing. An emergency loan smashes the price. Profits converted to EPS and dividends are the whole game in the end years.
  • Strategy label for the plan: ours maps to Capsim's "Broad Differentiator" archetype, run with cost discipline.

Executive Summary DRAFT v2

We will run this company as a Broad Differentiator with cost discipline. We compete in all five market segments. We aim to hold the best-rated product in each premium segment, the lowest cost base in the price-driven segments, and a balance sheet that never needs emergency funding.

Our advantage compounds over time. Sustained investment in people (HR), quality programs (TQM) and automation shortens our R&D cycle, lifts productivity and cuts unit costs. Faster R&D produces better-rated products. Better products earn above-average share and margin. Those profits fund the next round of investment and, from Year 2, a growing stream of dividends and buybacks. The simulation rewards exactly this loop: its share price model values earnings per share, book value and dividends, and nothing else.

The plan in one sentence: invest hard in Years 1 to 3, lead the market in Years 3 to 5, and harvest in Years 6 and 7, finishing with the industry's highest cumulative profit, a share price supported by real earnings, and a clean company for the next management team.

§1 Baseline Analysis: the industry at Round 0 and what it means DRAFT v2 (Akshaya to personalise)

Starting position. The industry begins 2026 as six identical companies created from a monopoly break-up. Each sold $101.1M, earned $4.2M of profit, and holds the same five products, one per segment. Nobody starts ahead: the race is decided entirely by decisions from Round 1 onward.

The market we compete in. Total demand is about 22,800 units in 2026 and grows quickly: Traditional 9.9% a year, Low End 12.6%, High End 16.9%, Performance 20.9% and Size 19.0%. The premium segments are small today but compound fastest, which rewards early investment in product quality and capacity. Price ranges fall $0.50 in every segment every year, so margins must come from cost reduction and share, not price rises.

What the baseline tells us about our own company. Baker is well placed in Traditional but ageing (3.1 years against an ideal of 2.0). Bead is a healthy Low End cash engine but carries reliability cost its customers do not value. Bid, Bold and Buddy are under-scaled in the three fastest-growing segments, with capacity of 900, 600 and 600 and automation of 3.0. The plant runs at 67 to 130% utilisation, leaving clear room to grow supply before buying capacity.

Implications for Round 1 (the lessons we bring from practice).

  • Commit immediately. Reposition every product to its 2027 ideal in the first save; late movers give away months of demand score.
  • Respect the ranges. A price or spec outside segment expectations does not reduce sales, it eliminates them (the rough cut).
  • Invest before it looks necessary. Automation on the volume lines and reliability upgrades in the premium lines pay back inside three rounds, and faster here than in practice given the growth rates.
  • Fund it properly. Plant investment is financed with long-term bonds plus a small cash buffer, keeping closing cash at 8% of sales so an emergency loan is never possible.

§4a to §4c Functional strategies DRAFT v2 (owners to personalise)

§4a R&D and Product strategy (Ben).

Objective: hold the highest customer survey score in High End, Performance and Size every December, and keep every product inside its segment's fine cut all year.

  • Every product is repositioned to its segment's new ideal spot every year. Projects must finish inside the calendar year: set the ideal, then pull back in 0.1 steps until the completion date fits December. A project that crosses the year boundary locks the product out of further revision and is not acceptable.
  • Low End is the deliberate exception. Its customers value old products (ideal age 7 years), so the product is left to age and is reset only when it ages past the peak. Its reliability spec sits at the bottom of the range to minimise material cost in a segment where price is 53% of the decision.
  • Reliability specs sit at the top of the range in Performance (43% of the buying decision) and High End, and at the floor in Low End. Each 1,000 hours of MTBF costs about $0.30 per unit in materials, so every spec is a priced decision.
  • HR scientist funding and the TQM engineering programs (Concurrent Engineering, QFD) cut project times by 20% or more. This is the enabling investment for the whole product strategy: it lets us reach next year's ideal while rivals sit at this year's.
  • Round 1 decision: whether to launch a second High End product. It takes about two years to build and would sell from Year 3 into a segment growing 16.9% a year. If approved it must be started in the first save.

§4b Marketing strategy (Akshaya).

Objective: convert product quality into demand by keeping awareness and accessibility climbing toward 100%, pricing to each segment's psychology, and forecasting accurately enough that production never guesses.

  • Pricing follows the buying criteria weights. High End, Performance and Size weigh price at only 9 to 19%, so we price at or near the top of the range and take the margin. Low End weighs price at 53%, so we stay within $0.50 of the cheapest credible rival. Traditional (23%) is priced competitively but never at the bottom. All prices step down as the published ranges fall $0.50 each year. No price ever leaves its range.
  • Promotion and sales budgets run at about $1,400 per volume product and $1,200 per niche product each year. The survey score is multiplied by both awareness and accessibility factors, so these budgets protect every point the products earn. Spend beyond $1,500 hits diminishing returns and is avoided.
  • A/R terms are set to 60 days from Round 1, cutting the demand score penalty from 5% (at 30 days) to 1.5%. We revisit longer terms when cash is deep.
  • Forecasts are built from segment size, growth and our expected share of potential demand, cross-checked against the Courier's potential-sold figures each round. Forecast misses above 10% are reviewed and explained at the next meeting.

§4c Production strategy (Rohit).

Objective: never lose a sale to a stockout on a premium product, and drive unit costs down every year through automation and utilisation.

  • Schedules equal best-case demand (forecast plus about 10%) minus inventory on hand, grossed up for the payment-terms adjustment. A small inventory carry costs a few percent; a stockout hands whole customers to rivals in a market where demand splits by score spread.
  • Automation rises early on the volume lines (Traditional and Low End first), because each point cuts labour cost by roughly 10% and pays back inside two to three rounds. Automation on frequently repositioned products is raised more carefully, as it slows R&D projects.
  • Capacity is bought one year ahead of demand growth (it arrives the following year). Target utilisation is 90 to 180% including second shift; sustained running above 180% triggers a capacity purchase.
  • Fire-sale rule: if a line is ever discontinued, capacity is sold down to one unit in the exit year and the last unit the following year. Selling 100% in one year forces the simulation to liquidate remaining inventory at half cost.
  • Workforce: training and assembly-team investment through HR keeps turnover low and the productivity index rising, which lowers effective labour cost every year.

§4d Finance Strategy DRAFT v2, copy-ready

Objective. Our finance strategy has three clear goals, in this order:

  • 1. Never run out of cash. We will not take an emergency loan at any point in the seven years.
  • 2. Fully fund the operating plan. R&D, plant and marketing get the money they need in Years 1 to 3, when investment earns the most.
  • 3. Grow shareholder returns. Earnings per share, dividends and the share price should rise every year from Year 2.

Assumptions this strategy relies on.

  • Plant investment (automation and capacity) of roughly $25M to $35M is needed across Years 1 to 3. After that, spending drops sharply.
  • Borrowing costs run at about 10% to 14% depending on our credit rating. A better rating means cheaper debt, so the rating is worth protecting.
  • The simulation rewards a leverage ratio (assets divided by equity) between 1.8 and 2.8. Outside that band we lose points.
  • The share price is driven mainly by earnings per share, book value and dividends. A dividend is only valued up to the level of earnings per share.
  • Profits from Year 2 onward are large enough to fund most spending internally, so heavy borrowing is only needed early.

The five rules we will follow.

  • 1. Match the loan to the asset. Long-term bonds pay for plant and equipment. Short-term (current) debt covers working capital only. We do not use short-term money for long-term assets.
  • 2. Keep a cash safety floor. Closing cash stays at 8% of forecast sales or higher, every year. Before saving each round we test the plan against sales coming in 10% below forecast. The floor must survive that test.
  • 3. Pay shareholders once profits allow. Start a dividend in Year 2. Grow it with earnings, paying out 15% to 40% of earnings per share. Buy back shares when we have surplus cash and low leverage.
  • 4. Manage leverage on purpose. As profits build up equity, leverage naturally falls. We hold it in the 1.8 to 2.8 band using dividends and buybacks. We do not retire cheap debt to do this, because that pushes leverage the wrong way.
  • 5. Check before every save. Four tests must pass: all R&D projects finish inside the year, leverage is in the band, cash is above the floor, and profit is on target. Interest costs stay under 4.5% of sales.

If things go wrong. If sales miss badly, the cash floor absorbs the first hit and we cut production, not price, in the next round. If our credit rating falls, we pause plant spending until it recovers. If a rival starts a price war, we defend only in the price-sensitive segments and take margin in the rest.

Evidence: this exact approach was tested over eight practice rounds. Result: zero emergency loans, leverage in or near the band every year, and a share price that rose about five times while funding the largest plant program in the industry.

§2 Business Model and Strategy to Win DRAFT v2, copy-ready

Our strategy in one line. We are a Broad Differentiator with cost discipline: we compete in all five segments, win the premium segments on product quality and reliable supply, win Low End on cost and product age, and convert the profits into shareholder returns.

The six levers, and how we push each one to its maximum.

  • 1. Product quality. Every product is moved to its segment's ideal spot every year and the update finishes inside the year. Maximised by investing in HR scientists and TQM engineering programs, which cut R&D time by 20% or more. This lets us reach positions rivals cannot reach in a single year. It is a compounding advantage: faster R&D leads to better products, which fund more investment.
  • 2. Demand multipliers. The demand score is multiplied by awareness and accessibility. We push both toward 100% on volume products with steady promotion and sales budgets, plus the Channel Support program. We also set A/R terms to 60 days from Round 1, cutting a hidden demand-score penalty from 5% (at 30 days) to 1.5%. When cash is deep in later years we can go longer and remove it entirely.
  • 3. Supply. We forecast from potential demand, not last year's sales, and hold a 10% production cushion. A stockout hands customers to rivals; leftover stock costs a few percent. We buy capacity and automation early (Years 1 to 3) so supply never caps our best products.
  • 4. Cost. Automation on the volume lines first, TQM cost programs from mid-game, and reliability specs set to the bottom of the range in price-driven segments. Target: contribution margin climbs from about 28% to 38% or better by Year 7.
  • 5. Cash and capital. The cash floor and matched funding keep us out of emergency loans. From Year 2 profits flow back to shareholders as dividends and buybacks, which is exactly what the share price formula rewards.
  • 6. Intelligence. We read the Courier as a weapon every round: rival stockouts are free customers, rivals locked in long R&D projects are free share, and mispriced products are free margin. Demand splits in proportion to survey scores, so widening the gap between our score and theirs is worth as much as raising our own. Decisions are locked hours before each deadline.

Timing plan across the seven years.

  • Years 1 to 2, commit and build: reposition everything, buy automation on volume lines, start HR and TQM, accept modest profit. Decide on a second High End product in Round 1 (two years to build, sells from Year 3).
  • Years 3 to 5, lead: best product in each segment, supply matched to potential, margins expanding, dividends growing.
  • Years 6 to 7, harvest: no new plant, maximum production, maximum shareholder returns, balance sheet clean for the next management team.

What could beat us, and our response. A rival who copies the product-quality game: we out-invest them early, because the time-lag effects mean the first mover keeps the lead. A Low End price war: we have the lowest costs, so we can follow price down and still profit, or hold price and bank margin when capacity is tight.
